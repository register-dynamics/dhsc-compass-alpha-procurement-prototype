Wrapping up the alpha phase

Date: March 2026 | Phase: Alpha Sprint 6

"We swept user research transcripts to identify remaining pain points and make sure feedback was reflected in the prototype ahead of handover to the beta team. We also created experimental dashboards and data visualisations to test ways of contextualising search results, and a new flow for sharing different types of documents."

The problem

After completing our alpha service assessment, we were given two sprints to wrap up and prepare a final handover for the beta team. We knew there were key iterations from recent user research rounds and earlier feedback that we had not yet had time to action.

We used our remaining time to address these from a user-centred design perspective, so that the prototype was in good shape for the team picking it up next.

Key design decisions

1. Updated search filters following IA discussion

After reviewing what we actually know at this stage, we decided to hold back advanced filter options. We do not yet have sufficient evidence to implement them well, and introducing filters prematurely risks creating a misleading sense of precision.

2. Experimental search results views with data visualisation

We created experimental views of the search results page that incorporate data visualisations — including supplier market share and adoption across similar trusts — to test whether additional context helps users shortlist with more confidence.

3. Searching by care setting as an additional entry point

We introduced care setting as a new way to jump into the service, giving users a more clinical starting point alongside category and keyword search.

4. New step in the sharing flow to specify document type

We added a choice screen early in the sharing flow, allowing users to indicate whether they are uploading an evaluation or a business case before proceeding. This reduces ambiguity and enables more appropriate downstream routing.

5. Visuals to support understanding of product pages

After hearing that users expected and wanted to see product imagery, we added a product image to the product page to aid recognition and give the page a more grounded, real-world feel.

6. Stripped-back homepage content

Based on consistent user feedback about information density, we reduced the content on the homepage to its essentials. Users were not reading the longer explanatory text, and its presence was slowing orientation.

Content updates

Alongside structural changes, we made a range of language-focused content improvements across the prototype informed by user research findings and legal guidance.

Reducing information density

We further reduced text throughout the prototype, particularly on the homepage and on product and evaluation cards, responding to recurring feedback that users felt overwhelmed by content.

Surfacing personal activity

Saved items and recent searches are now more prominently displayed on the search page within a dedicated "Your activity" area, making it easier for returning users to pick up where they left off.

Accessibility and legibility

We increased the contrast of text that a user found difficult to read during a research session.

Country of manufacture

We added a "country of manufacture" row to product cards following legal advice. The previous design risked inadvertently promoting UK-manufactured products above others, which was not our intention.

Clarifying adoption numbers

The "using this" count on product cards was updated to better reflect what it actually represents. Users were confused by the number, which does not necessarily match procurement outcomes or uploaded documents.

Action-oriented copy

We reviewed copy across the service to ensure it was clear, concise, and accurate. For example, we updated language around the evaluation lead role to reflect that this is not always a clinician.

Contextualising the dashboard

We added clarifying copy to the dashboard to give users the additional context they wanted around what it means to search and share on the service.

Service name

We updated the service name following user feedback gathered across several name options tested in research sessions.

Source for cost data

We added a source citation for cost data on product pages to give users confidence in where figures come from.

Multiple answers on the uploading page

We addressed a need identified in research for users to be able to give multiple answers to the procurement outcome question, and added a follow-up question for those who did not end up procuring a product.

Terminology for "evaluation"

In some parts of the service, we updated the word "evaluation" to alternative terms such as experience, contribution, and document. Research showed that "evaluation" carries a specific meaning for some users — typically a formal clinical evaluation — that did not always match how we were using the term on the service.

How it tested

Information:

Final testing sessions revealed some additional insights:

  • Some users didn't realise there were more sections to explore in the body of the search page.
  • One user wanted to be able to see similar products once they've drilled down to the single product level. For example, when navigating directly to a specific insulin pump, they wanted to see alternatives to be sure of their choice.
  • Users weren't necessarily interested in supplier size or maturity.
  • Users found the visualisations we tested on the search results page interesting in theory but weren't sure about the usefullness of the information presented.
  • Specific mention of "procurement" made at least one clinician feel this service wasn't for them.

Content areas to prioritise in beta

Accessibility

A full accessibility review is needed, including finding a way to make PDFs that users may upload and share accessible. This should test a range of approaches: service-side and user-side manual adjustments, format conversion tools, and AI-assisted options.

Subcategory visualisation and upload journeys

Further testing of the subcategory data visualisation is needed, alongside business case and product evaluation-specific upload journeys — including whether a narrower definition of document types would better serve users.

Information architecture

Formal IA work including tree testing and card sorting would help validate the current structure and surface where users are getting lost or confused.

Search results and filters

In-depth work on search results and filters was deliberately deferred from alpha. This is a significant area of design work for beta, particularly around what filters are useful and how results should be ranked and presented.

Product page hierarchy

The product pages are long and contain a lot of information. Hierarchy work is needed to establish the right order for categories and cards, and to ensure the most decision-relevant content is prominent.

Content management planning

Longer-term planning is needed around how content on the service will be managed, maintained, and kept accurate over time.
